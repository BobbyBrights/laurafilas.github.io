Sometimes it can be hard to find good music.
Fifteen Twelve Seventeen
 is a yearly project curated and designed by Laura Filas in an effort to bring more attention to some of the good music that might’ve otherwise been missed. Each album is accompanied by a poster capturing its essence. So, stop the search and break out your head phones or maybe even buy the records.
15 Sunrise
Calico Blue
Velvet
If there’s a simple way to describe this album, it’s that it is rich in texture. Calico Blue blend psychedelic rock and blues-y surf rock together seamlesly to form a completely fresh and simultaneously revived sound. Jangly guitars and velvety percussion juxtapose lead vocalist, Sarah Addi’s lush and mysterious crooning to compliment an album urgent, yet full of hope and light creeping in.
Charlemont Reef
Sagamore
The Bridge
If you’re unfamiliar with the band Sagamore, you’ll never believe that Charlemont Reef is their debut full-length album. Already giving off a sense of maturity and direction that is usually only achieved by a band’s third album, the 5-piece crafts a burst of hazy energy peppered in upbeat guitar riffs, soulful vocals, and artful horns within the constraints of nine tracks. A hybrid of psychedelic rock and roots, the album is recieved just as the sweet, warm night air coming through your window at night sometime in June.
Somersault
Beach Fossils
May 1st
After a four-year hiatus, Beach Fossils are finally back with the much anticipated Somersault. Reshaping their signature surf rock/dream pop combo into something transcendent and even more nuanced than ever before, the boys prove that they are able to push themselves into territory beyond the comfort zone of the clouds hanging over a hazy summer’s day without losing the recipe for what made their songs so special and effortless from the beginning. Simply put, they’ve added string arrangements, a flute solo, some saxaphone, and a feature from Cities Aviv––producing a landscape of sound that can’t compare to anything else.
Sugar at the Gate
TOPS
Marigold & Gray
TOPS’ third album Sugar at the Gate, beams with Rumours-esque glam and a marriage of some type of mysterious disco and 80s synth pop. Though Sugar at the Gate isn’t a gutting of past endeavors, but rather something of
a careful refinement, the vocals manage to effortlessly reel you into a euphoric pool of pastel sounds just as well as past albums.
And once you start listening, it’s pretty difficult to stop.
Aron D'alesio
Aron D'alesio
Wrong End of a Knife
The frontman of Ontario-based band Young Rival, Aron D’alesio is no stranger to ‘60s rock influence––and you can definitely hear it on his debut, solo record. It is best listened to crackiling on vinyl in the dark of a winter night or, you know, any other time you please. A satisfying blend of the warmth of nostalgia and the excitement of something refreshing, the album is impossible not to fall in love with.
Outside (Briefly)
Froth
Shatter
Nothing short of their signature brand of fuzzed-out shoegaze, Froth craft a record that remains dreamy, yet strays far from psychedelic predecessors or contemporaries. Marbled in both slow, winding, and light tracks and heavier, krautrock-laced pieces, Outside (Briefly) flows lazily and facilely like an unpredictable journey your ear can’t help but follow. Pretty impressive for a band that started out as a prank a few years back.
Ti Amo
Phoenix
Role Model
Well, this album is probably a lot more talked about than any of the others on this list, so let’s keep it short. This is French rock group, Phoenix’s sixth album and it’s everything you could ever want and more. A bursting ray of sunshine-y synth pop, soft rock, and disco drench you in bliss. It’s simply an exceptional album and you must listen to it.
Hang
Foxygen
Follow the Leader
Only eight songs long (oh man), but quite a star. Foxygen’s latest endeavor almost completely abandons their past sound in place of the theatrical grandoisity of ‘70s glam rock, remniscent of Hunky Dory-era Bowie. However much it points to the past, it is still infused with something unexpected. Artful and full of pomp and confidence (and an entire orchestra), it hits your ears hard. Something you have to listen to a few times to truly understand the beauty, absurdity, and satire it carries. If you do spend the time with Hang, the reward you reap is almost as grand as the album itself.
Please Be Mine
Molly Burch
Try
A collection of love songs has never sounded so good. Please Be Mine is another debut that sounds far too sophisticated to be a debut. What’s even more unbelievable? It
was tracked live in only one day. Though all ten tracks revolve around one topic, they are anything but monotonous. Burch’s sultry, plush voice drapes over retro reverb-drenched Americana remniscent of some cinematic endeavor from the ‘50s or ’60s. Where the music is slow, the vocals are charged with swirling emotion and steal the show (and your heart) and where it picks up, Burch’s voice strikes through with a rare, remarkable force.
City Music
Kevin Morby
City Music
City Music is Kevin Morby’s fourth album and arguably the most beautiful one. A gentle, melancholic, hopeful, naïve, narrative full of poetic wisdoms of romantic notions and the dislocation, isolation, and distress of city life, City Music brings to attention the lack of hope in humanity we might face in today’s political climate while simultaneously eliciting glimmers of optimism within our hearts. The record is a feat not many contemporaries could pull off. Raw and powerful and haun-ting, the record shines and leaves you feeling alright and transformed for the better.
Wildly Idle (Humble Before The Void)
Hand Habits
Nite Life
Wildly Idle (Humble Before The Void) is a soft, yet powerful album which steals you away to soar high above a vast, empty American desert, striking you with your insignificance as you look down below at the chimerical land-scape. Meg Duffy’s vocals are so arresting you won’t want to move or speak or whisper or even take a breath to disrupt the phenomenal beauty of what you’re hearing and the inst-rumentation lines your journey with a sonic quality both immensely calming and midly eerie. Truly a rare and tenderly captivating gem, it will leave you overwhelmed in the most satisfying way possible and maybe a little teary-eyed.
Routines
Hoops
Sun's Out
If there’s one lo-fi/dream pop album that you simply must listen to, it’s this one. Routines is not necessarily shocking, but it tends to stray from that which is so overdone within the genre. A blissed-out collection of eleven tracks inspired by the ‘80s, the album is crow-ded by lush, hazy guitar riffs, warm vintage synths, and of course, the most interesting vocals. Pink-orange sunsets, roadtrips to the beach, and warm breezes come to mind when you listen to this record. And no matter what, your ears never grow tired of hearing it all.
The Babe Rainbow
The Babe Rainbow
Survival Into the 21st Century
Well, let’s take a trip back to the kaleidescopic late ‘60s. Floating through an alternate time and space is where you will find yourself when listening through songs with playfully strange titles like “Peace Blossom Boogy”. Lofty but not aloof, the sounds of The Babe Rainbow conjugate a time that has passed with the present and shape it––as effortlessly as you can play-doh––into something fresh and charming. Feathery vocals softly drift into groovy guitar solos, remniscent of sunny days on the beach, only this beach is far off and doused in an unusual wash of pulsing colors.
Faye Webster
Faye Webster
She Won't Go Away
A remarkably vulnerable and breathtaking brooding of Americana (on a rap label), Faye Webster’s self-titled album is no debut, but her sophomore effort. No sophomore slump here though. Webster charms with her cosmic voice weaving a web of dazzling sound, impo-ssible not to resonate with. Each song tells a story and each story drowns you in a satiny sea of absolute delight.
Beauty & Sadness
Horsebeach
I Must Work & I Must Die
The title, Beauty & Sadness reveals exactly what this album is. It’s a mosaic of beauty and melancholy, but it’s hard to feel sad when listening to it. Maybe beauty and sadness aren’t complete opposites, but they are surely juxtaposed well here. Horsebeach falls into a whirl of shoegaze and the jangling guitars of C-86, floating in the smoothness of reverb-soaked sounds, at times groovy bass lines, and hazy synths. Feelings of longing and sadness press against happy sounds and emit an airy quality––easy on the ears and sugge-stive of a roadtrip to somewhere sunny, or you know, rainy.
